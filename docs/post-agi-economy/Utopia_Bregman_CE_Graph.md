## **CENTRAL THESIS**

**We are richer than ever, yet lack a compelling vision for the future. The book proposes three interconnected utopias—shorter work week, universal basic income, and open borders—all feasible now, supported by evidence, blocked mainly by outdated ideas.**

## **I. THE 15-HOUR WORKWEEK**

### **Claim 1.1: We predicted shorter work weeks a century ago, and had good reason to**

**Evidence:**
- **Keynes (1930):** Predicted 15-hour workweek by 2030 based on productivity trends
- **Historical precedent:** Between 1850–1980, work hours *did* steadily decline
  - English farmer (1300): ~1,500 hours/year
  - Industrial worker (1850s): ~3,000 hours/year  
  - By 1930: steady decline underway
- **Henry Ford (1926):** Implemented 5-day, 40-hour week; productivity *increased*
- **U.S. Senate (1933):** Passed 30-hour workweek bill (died in House)

### **Claim 1.2: Productivity growth continued, but work hours stopped falling after ~1980**

**Evidence:**
- **U.S. median real wage (1969–2009):** Declined 14% despite rising productivity
- **Work-hour stagnation:** 
  - Australia, Austria, Norway, Spain, England: workweeks stopped shrinking post-1980
  - U.S.: actually *increased* for many workers
- **Family time compression:** 
  - 1950s couples: 5–6 combined workdays/week  
  - 2000s couples: 7–8 days/week
  - Women joined workforce; men didn't reduce hours proportionally
- **Time pressure rising:** 
  - Netherlands (1985–2005): weekly obligations rose from 43.6 → 48.6 hours
  - Smartphone: average employee works +460 hours/year (≈3 extra weeks)

### **Claim 1.3: Shorter hours don't harm productivity—they often boost it**

**Evidence:**
- **W.K. Kellogg (1930), Battle Creek, Michigan:**  
  - Introduced 6-hour day
  - Results: hired 300 more workers, accident rate ↓41%, unit cost of production fell enough to pay same wages for fewer hours
  - Workers reported vastly improved quality of life (more civic participation, family time)
- **Edward Heath's 3-day week (UK, 1974):**  
  - Energy crisis forced firms to operate only 3 days/week
  - Expected production loss: 50%  
  - Actual loss: **6%**
- **Correlation data (OECD):**  
  - Countries with *shorter* workweeks show *higher* productivity per hour
  - Knowledge workers productive max ~6 hours/day (creative work)
- **Apple (1980s):** Employees worked 90-hour weeks; productivity experts later calculated they'd have shipped the Macintosh *a year earlier* at 45 hours/week

### **Claim 1.4: Shorter work solves multiple problems at once**

**Evidence:**

| **Problem** | **Evidence shorter work helps** |
|-------------|----------------------------------|
| **Stress & burnout** | Studies: people working less report higher life satisfaction |
| **Climate change** | Modeling: global shift to shorter week could cut CO₂ emissions this century by ~50%; countries with shorter weeks have smaller ecological footprints |
| **Accidents** | Long hours = more errors (tired surgeons, soldiers, Chernobyl, Challenger disaster often involved overwork) |
| **Unemployment** | ILO: work-sharing softened last crisis; distributes available work more broadly |
| **Gender equality** | Countries with short workweeks top gender-equality rankings; paternity leave has lasting effects (Norway: men who take leave do 50% more laundry for life) |
| **Aging population** | Redistributes work across generations; allows older workers to stay employed part-time |
| **Inequality** | Countries with biggest wealth gaps have *longest* workweeks |

## **II. UNIVERSAL BASIC INCOME (UBI)**

### **Claim 2.1: Poverty is fundamentally a lack of cash, not a character flaw**

**Evidence:**
- **London homeless experiment (2009):**  
  - 13 rough sleepers, on streets up to 40 years  
  - Given £3,000 each, no strings attached  
  - After 18 months: 7 had roofs, 2 moving into housing, all enrolled in classes/rehab/skills training  
  - Average spent: only £800  
  - Cost: £50,000/year (including staff) vs. previous £400,000/year in police, courts, services
- **Give Directly (Kenya):**  
  - Cash transfers ($500) to poor villagers  
  - Results (MIT study): incomes ↑38%, home ownership & livestock ↑58%, child hunger days ↓42%  
  - 93% of donations reached recipients directly
- **Uganda (2008):**  
  - ~12,000 youth given ~$400 + business plan requirement  
  - 5 years later: incomes ↑50%, employment odds ↑60%
- **Uganda women (north):**  
  - 1,800 poor women given $150  
  - Incomes ↑100%  
  - Adding a aid worker ($350 cost) helped only slightly; better to give cash

### **Claim 2.2: The poor do *not* waste money on vice; they invest it**

**Evidence:**
- **World Bank meta-analysis:**  
  - 82% of studied cases in Africa, Latin America, Asia: alcohol & tobacco consumption *declined* after cash transfers
- **Liberia experiment:**  
  - Gave $200 to "worst" candidates: alcoholics, addicts, petty criminals  
  - 3 years later: money spent on food, clothing, medicine, small businesses  
  - Researcher: "If *these* men didn't waste free money, who would?"
- **The Lancet (2013):** Reviewed evidence; poor given no-strings cash tend to *work harder*

### **Claim 2.3: Cash transfers have huge multiplier effects, especially for children**

**Evidence:**
- **Cherokee casino payments (North Carolina, 1997–):**  
  - Eastern Band Cherokee: ~8,000 members get rising annual payments (started $500/year, grew to $6,000 by 2001)
  - Duke University study (Jane Costello), 1993–present:
    - Behavioral problems in lifted-out-of-poverty children ↓40%
    - Juvenile crime, drug/alcohol use ↓
    - School performance matched non-tribal peers
    - Effect strongest for youngest cohort
    - Extra $4,000/year = +1 year education by age 21, 22% less criminal record at 16
    - **Mechanism:** Not less parental work, but *less financial stress* → better parenting
- **Greg Duncan (UC Irvine):**  
  - Calculated lifting a U.S. family out of poverty costs ~$4,500/year  
  - ROI per child by middle age:
    - 12.5% more hours worked  
    - $3,000/year savings on welfare  
    - $50,000–100,000 additional lifetime earnings  
    - $10,000–20,000 additional state tax revenue  
  - **"Pays for itself by middle age"**
- **Randall Akee (UCLA):** Cherokee program's cost savings (via reduced crime, repetition, care use) exceeded total payouts

### **Claim 2.4: Poverty taxes cognitive bandwidth ("mental bandwidth")**

**Evidence:**
- **Shafir & Mullainathan experiments:**
  1. **Mall study (U.S.):**  
     - Shoppers asked about hypothetical car repair ($150 vs. $1,500)  
     - Poor scored same as rich on cognitive tests when repair was cheap  
     - Poor scored *much worse* when repair was expensive  
     - Conclusion: *thinking about* financial stress impairs cognition
  2. **Indian sugarcane farmers:**  
     - Tested pre-harvest (poor) and post-harvest (flush with cash)  
     - Same people, same tests  
     - Scored significantly *worse* when poor—not because they're dumber, but because bandwidth is taxed
- **Effect size:** Poverty's cognitive impact ≈ **13–14 IQ points** (comparable to losing a night's sleep or effects of alcoholism)
- **Mechanism:** Scarcity (time, money, etc.) creates tunnel vision, short-term focus, worse decisions
  - Not stupidity; it's **context**

### **Claim 2.5: Basic income experiments show people do NOT stop working**

**Evidence:**
- **Mincome (Dauphin, Canada, 1974–1979):**  
  - Entire town guaranteed income floor (family of 4: ~$19,000/year in 2016 dollars)  
  - Work hours ↓ only slightly:
    - Men: 1%  
    - Married women: 3%  
    - Unmarried women: 5%  
  - Who reduced hours: new mothers (maternity leave), students (stayed in school longer)
  - Hospitalizations ↓8.5%  
  - Domestic violence ↓, mental health complaints ↓  
  - Effects persisted into next generation (earnings, health)
- **U.S. experiments (1968–1980s):** New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Iowa, North Carolina, Indiana, Seattle, Denver; 8,500+ participants  
  - Paid work ↓ ~9% per family (later corrected to even less when income under-reporting was accounted for)  
  - Reductions mainly: youth (more school), mothers (more childcare)  
  - One mother: dropped out of high school, used time to earn psych degree, became researcher  
  - New Jersey: high school graduation ↑30%
- **Nixon's FAP (Family Assistance Plan):**  
  - Passed House twice (1970: 243–155; 1971: 288–132)  
  - Died in Senate (Democrats wanted *more*, Republicans feared it; final kill came when Seattle study *incorrectly* showed divorce spike—later debunked as statistical error)

### **Claim 2.6: Current welfare systems are expensive, demeaning, and counterproductive**

**Evidence:**
- **Bureaucratic costs:** Means-tested programs require massive admin (caseworkers, inspectors, paperwork)
- **Perverse incentives:** Benefits withdrawn as you earn → poverty trap
- **Orwell's observation (1933):** Welfare "annihilates the future"; claimants must repeatedly *prove* incapacity to get help
- **Scholarship non-take-up:** ~30% of eligible students don't apply for scholarships worth thousands, despite huge ROI—bandwidth problem
- **Financial education programs:** Meta-analysis of 201 studies shows they make almost no difference (Shafir: "like teaching someone to swim, then throwing them in a stormy sea")

### **Claim 2.7: We can afford it**

**Evidence:**
- **U.S. poverty line (Matt Bruenig):** Eradicating poverty in U.S. would cost **~$175 billion/year**  
  - ≈¼ of U.S. military budget  
  - A bargain vs. Iraq/Afghanistan wars ($4–6 trillion, Harvard study)
- **Child poverty in U.S.:** Costs ~$500 billion/year in lost productivity, health, crime
- **British study:** Child poverty costs UK £29B/year; eliminating it "could largely pay for itself"
- **Funding mechanisms:** Simplify/eliminate existing benefits and tax credits; tax wealth, pollution, carbon, financial transactions

## **III. AUTOMATION & THE FUTURE OF WORK**

### **Claim 3.1: Machines are eating jobs faster than ever ("Second Machine Age")**

**Evidence:**
- **Historical precedent:**  
  - Draft horses: England had 1M+ in 1901; nearly extinct by mid-century due to cars/tractors
  - Leontief (1983): "Role of humans… will diminish in the same way that the role of horses… was eliminated"
- **The Great Decoupling (post-2000):**  
  - For decades, productivity growth ≈ job growth  
  - After ~2000: productivity continues rising, job growth stagnates (U.S. Dept. of Labor data)
  - MIT economists: "Productivity at record levels… yet falling median income and fewer jobs"
- **Oxford study (2013):**  
  - 47% of U.S. jobs at high risk of automation in next 20 years  
  - 54% in Europe
- **Examples:**
  - 2004: Experts said driving could *never* be automated  
  - 2010: Google self-driving cars logged a million miles  
  - IBM Watson (2011): beat trivia champions on *Jeopardy!*  
  - Kodak (1980s): 145,000 employees; bankrupt 2012  
  - Instagram (2012): 13 employees, sold for $1B

### **Claim 3.2: Labor's share of income is shrinking; capital's is growing**

**Evidence:**
- **Kaldor's "constant" (1957):** 2/3 of income to labor, 1/3 to capital—supposedly fixed  
- **Reality (today):** Labor's share in developed countries ≈ 58% (down from ~67%)
- **Winner-take-all dynamics:**  
  - 1964: Top 4 U.S. firms averaged 430,000 employees  
  - 2011: Top 4 averaged ¼ that, worth 2× as much  
  - Globalization + digitization → small quality edge = total dominance

### **Claim 3.3: Standard responses (more education, austerity) won't solve this**

**Evidence:**
- **Education limitations:**  
  - "Low-hanging fruit" already picked (basic literacy/numeracy was huge ROI; further gains are expensive, slow)
  - Credentialism rising, but many degrees don't guarantee good jobs
  - Geographic/educational sorting: educated cluster together; gap between most/least educated cities doubled since 1970s
- **The Luddites were partly right:**  
  - 1811–1830s: British textile workers lost jobs to machines; wages collapsed; uprising crushed  
  - Threshing machines destroyed thousands of jobs instantly  
  - Agricultural production ↑33% (1790–1830), but fewer people could afford food  
  - Problem wasn't *laziness*; it was *technological unemployment* + no redistribution mechanism

### **Claim 3.4: Only solution is redistribution: tax capital, give people money/time**

**Evidence:**
- **Thomas Piketty:** Return on capital (r) > growth (g) → inequality spirals unless checked by progressive wealth tax or catastrophe
- **Hypothetical interventions:**
  - Financial transactions tax (would curb HFT, raise revenue, redirect talent)
  - Wealth/capital taxes
  - Shorter workweek (spreads available work)
  - UBI (decouples survival from employment)
- **Historical parallel:** 19th-century response to industrialization was universal education + labor protections + welfare state; 21st century needs equivalent leap

## **IV. METRICS & PROGRESS (GDP Critique)**

### **Claim 4.1: GDP measures economic activity, not welfare**

**Evidence:**
- **What GDP counts:**
  - Disaster recovery (Japan 2011 earthquake: $235B damage → economy grew 2% in 2012)
  - Pollution cleanup (company pollutes, another cleans it up: double-counted as "growth")
  - Crime, divorce, illness (more lawyers, doctors, police = higher GDP)
  - Wars (WWII pulled U.S. out of Depression)
- **What GDP ignores:**
  - Unpaid work (childcare, cooking, volunteering = 37–74% of economy depending on country)
  - Breastfeeding (Denmark tried to count it; U.S. potential value: $110B/year)
  - Technological improvements (storage cost: $300K/GB in 1980s → $0.10 today; barely registers in GDP)
  - Inequality (GDP can grow while median income falls)
  - Free goods (Wikipedia, Skype, open-source software *lower* GDP)
  - Environmental depletion

### **Claim 4.2: GDP was invented for wartime, not welfare**

**Evidence:**
- **Simon Kuznets (1934):** Developed national income accounts to answer Depression-era question: "How much can we produce?"
- **WWII usage:** GDP became tool for coordinating war production; Allies used it to outproduce Axis
- **Kuznets' own warnings (1934, 1962):**  
  - "Welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from… national income"  
  - Advised *against* including military, advertising, finance in GDP  
  - "Goals for more growth should specify more growth of *what* and *for what*"
- **Post-war:** GDP became *the* metric; economists became dominant policy advisors; growth = unquestioned goal

### **Claim 4.3: We need a dashboard of metrics for a modern economy**

**Evidence:**
- **RFK (1968):** GDP "measures everything except that which makes life worthwhile"
- **Alternatives proposed:**
  - Bhutan: Gross National Happiness (flawed: ignores dictatorship, ethnic cleansing)
  - Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) / Index of Sustainable Economic Welfare (ISEW): adjust for pollution, inequality, crime, volunteer work
    - In U.S./Europe, GPI flat or falling since 1970s even as GDP rose
  - Happy Planet Index, OECD Better Life Index, UN Human Development Index
- **Baumol's Cost Disease reframed:**
  - As productivity rises in manufacturing, *should* spend higher % on care/education/arts (low-productivity, high-value services)
  - We wrongly see rising costs in these sectors as "disease" rather than blessing
  - Example: String quartet in 1780 needed 4 musicians; still needs 4 today—no productivity gain possible, but *that's fine*

### **Claim 4.4: Measuring the wrong things drives the wrong policies**

**Evidence:**
- **Financial sector (pre-2008):**  
  - Statisticians measured bank "productivity" by *risk-taking*  
  - More risk = higher GDP contribution  
  - By 2008, UK banks = 9% of GDP (≈ entire manufacturing); catastrophic when it crashed
- **Performance targets in public sector:**  
  - Schools: high graduation rate ≠ good education  
  - Hospitals: "efficient" care ≠ good health outcomes  
  - Policing: arrest quotas ≠ safety  
  - Publishing: citation counts ≠ good science

## **V. OPEN BORDERS**

### **Claim 5.1: Migration is the single most powerful anti-poverty tool**

**Evidence:**
- **Economic modeling (7 studies):** Open borders would increase gross world product by **67–172%** (i.e., roughly double global wealth)
- **Individual income gains (World Bank):**  
  - Average Angolan moving to rich country: +$10,000/year  
  - Average Nigerian: +$22,000/year  
  - Mexican in U.S. earns 2× Mexican in Mexico (same person, same skills)  
  - American vs. Bolivian (same age/sex/skill): American earns 3×  
  - American vs. Nigerian: 8.5× (PPP-adjusted)
- **Compared to aid:**  
  - Total global development aid: $135B/year  
  - If rich countries admitted just 3% more immigrants: +$305B for world's poor (World Bank)  
  - Pritchett (Harvard): Removing all capital restrictions = $65B gain; removing labor restrictions = **$65 trillion**

### **Claim 5.2: Borders are the biggest source of global inequality**

**Evidence:**
- **Income distribution:**
  - Richest 8% earn half of world income  
  - Richest 1% own >half of wealth  
  - Poorest billion: 1% of consumption; richest billion: 72%
- **U.S. poverty line = global top 14%**  
- **U.S. median wage = global top 4%**
- **Goldman Sachs bonuses (2009) = combined income of world's 224 million poorest people**
- **62 richest people = poorest 3.5 billion people combined** (Oxfam, 2016)
- **Child mortality:** Somalian toddler has 20% chance of dying before age 5 (higher than death rate for U.S. frontline soldiers in Civil War, WWII, Vietnam)

### **Claim 5.3: Common anti-immigration arguments are empirically false**

| **Objection** | **Evidence** |
|---------------|--------------|
| *"They'll take our jobs"* | Labor market isn't fixed; more workers = more consumption = more jobs. Women entering workforce 1970s didn't displace men. |
| *"They'll lower wages"* | Center for Immigration Studies (anti-immigration think tank): immigration has *virtually no effect* on wages. Other research: slight *increase* in native wages. World Bank: emigration *out* of Europe (1990–2000) *hurt* low-skilled European wages. |
| *"They're lazy / drain welfare"* | Correcting for income/job status, immigrants use *less* public assistance than natives. Countries with strong safety nets don't attract higher immigrant shares. |
| *"They'll never leave"* | Paradox: *closed* borders trap people. Mexico–U.S. (1960s): 70M crossed, 85% returned. Post-9/11 militarization: now only 7% return. Circular migration becomes permanent when borders harden. |

### **Claim 5.4: Borders were historically open; passports are a modern invention**

**Evidence:**
- **Jules Verne, *Around the World in 80 Days* (1874):** Consul says passports are "only good for annoying honest folks… no passport is required"
- **Pre-WWI:** Borders existed on paper; passports rare (mainly in autocracies like Russia/Ottoman Empire)
- **1920 Paris conference:** First international agreements on passport use (post-WWI, to control spies and labor)
- **Today:** Only 3% of world population lives outside birth country (despite "globalization")
- **Policy:** Goods, services, capital move freely; labor does not

### **Claim 5.5: Even modest opening would have huge effects**

**Evidence:**
- **Poll (Gallup):** 700 million people would move if they could
- **Policy options:**
  - Conditional visas (must find work, pay taxes X years before benefits, pass language/culture test)
  - Temporary work permits
  - Gradual expansion
- **Historical analogy:** China (1990s–2000s): largest internal migration in history (hundreds of millions rural → urban); disruptive but drove massive growth

## **VI. HOW IDEAS CHANGE THE WORLD**

### **Claim 6.1: People resist changing deeply held beliefs (cognitive dissonance)**

**Evidence:**
- **Festinger's UFO cult (1954):**  
  - Dorothy Martin's group: predicted Dec 21 flood, aliens would rescue them  
  - Midnight came, nothing happened  
  - Group didn't disband; instead, received "new message": their faith saved Earth  
  - Began proselytizing *more* after disconfirmation
- **Cognitive dissonance:** When reality contradicts belief, we rationalize reality away, not belief
- **Modern examples:**
  - Educated people *more* rigid (better at finding confirming evidence)
  - Ezra Klein: "Smart people don't use intellect to obtain correct answer; they use it to obtain the answer they want"
- **2008 financial crisis:**  
  - Alan Greenspan (former Fed chair): "shocked disbelief," admitted "flaw" in ideology  
  - Few years later, reporter asked if he'd erred; Greenspan: "Not at all… no alternative"  
  - Banking sector largely unchanged; same risks, same bonuses

### **Claim 6.2: Crises are opportunities—but only if alternative ideas are ready**

**Evidence:**
- **Friedman's dictum:** "Only a crisis… produces real change. When that crisis occurs, the actions taken depend on the ideas that are lying around."
- **Neoliberal success story (1947–1980s):**
  - **Mont Pèlerin Society (1947):** Hayek, Friedman, 40 thinkers meet in Switzerland; feel "hopelessly out of tune"  
  - Spend decades building network, think tanks, policy proposals  
  - **1973 oil crisis** + stagflation = Keynesian theory can't explain  
  - Friedman's ideas "lying around," ready to deploy  
  - Result: Thatcher, Reagan, neoliberal revolution  
  - Thatcher's greatest achievement (her words): converting Labour Party (Tony Blair's "New Labour")
- **2008 crisis:**  
  - No comparable left/progressive alternative ready  
  - Result: bailouts, austerity, return to status quo  
  - Occupy fizzled; left parties lost elections across Europe

### **Claim 6.3: Single dissenting voices matter; conformity can be broken**

**Evidence:**
- **Asch conformity experiments (1950s):**  
  - Subjects asked which line is longest  
  - When confederates all gave wrong answer, subject conformed ~⅓ of the time  
  - **BUT:** When *one* other person gave correct answer, subject's conformity plummeted  
  - Lesson: You don't need a majority; you need *one ally*

### **Claim 6.4: Change is a long game; requires patience and "utopian courage"**

**Evidence:**
- **Hayek:** "It could easily take a generation before new ideas prevail… We need thinkers with courage to be 'utopian'"
- **Keynes:** "The difficulty lies not in the new ideas, but in escaping from the old ones"
- **Neoliberal trajectory:** 1947 (fringe cult) → 1970s (intellectual legitimacy) → 1980s (political dominance) ≈ **30–40 years**
- **Implication for UBI, short workweek, open borders:** Need long-term institution-building, not just crisis opportunism

## **SUPPORTING CROSS-CUTTING EVIDENCE**

### **Historical Progress (Chapter 1)**

**Claim:** We've come further in 200 years than in all prior history; pessimism is unjustified.

**Evidence:**
- **Extreme poverty:**  
  - 1820: 94% of world  
  - 1981: 44%  
  - 2016: <10%
- **Life expectancy:**  
  - 1900: ~31 years globally  
  - 1990: 64  
  - 2012: 70  
  - Africa gaining 4 days/week; rich countries gaining 1–2 days/week
- **Child mortality (since 1990):** Dropped from 1 in 5 → 1 in 20
- **Nutrition:** 1965: 51% <2,000 cal/day → 2005: 3%
- **Violence:** Murder rates in Europe ↓40× since Middle Ages; war deaths (per capita) ↓90% since 1946
- **Material wealth:** Average Italian 15× wealthier than in 1880; global economy 250× larger than pre-Industrial Revolution

**Implication:** We have the *material capacity* for utopia; lack of vision, not resources, is the constraint.

---

### **The Welfare State Paradox (Chapters 5, 6, 8)**

**Claim:** Helping people is cheaper than *not* helping them; yet we underfund prevention and overfund punishment/management.

**Evidence:**

| **Case** | **Cost of problem** | **Cost of solution** | **Savings** |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Homelessness (London, 13 rough sleepers)** | £400K/year (police, courts, services) | £50K/year (housing + support) | 8:1 |
| **Homelessness (Utah, statewide)** | $16,670/year per person (services, police, ER) | $11,000/year (apartment + counseling) | Chronic homelessness ↓74%; program saved money |
| **Homelessness (Netherlands, 2006–2014)** | — | — | ROI = 2–3× for every €1 invested |
| **Child poverty (U.S.)** | $500B/year (lost productivity, health, crime) | ~$175B/year (eradicate poverty) | Net positive |
| **Cherokee children** | — | $6,000/year per person | Savings (crime, health, schools) > payouts |

**Mechanism:** Prevention is investment; punishment/mitigation is expense.

### **The Bullshit-Jobs Hypothesis (Chapter 7)**

**Claim:** Many high-paid jobs create little or no social value; many essential jobs are poorly paid.

**Evidence:**
- **Sanitation workers (NYC, 1968):** 9-day strike paralyzed city; state of emergency declared
- **Bankers (Ireland, 1970):** 6-month strike; economy *continued to grow*; people used pubs as banks, wrote IOUs
- **Surveys:**
  - 37% of British workers think their job is "bullshit" (YouGov)
  - Harvard Business Review: 50% of 12,000 professionals say job has no "meaning and significance"
- **Social value vs. pay (New Economics Foundation, UK):**
  - Ad executive: For every £1 earned, destroys £7 in value (stress, overconsumption, pollution)
  - Trash collector: For every £1 earned, creates £12 in value (health, sustainability)
- **Financialization:**
  - 1950s: UK banks ≈0% of GDP  
  - 2008: 9% (≈all of manufacturing)
  - U.S. (1970 → 1990s): Harvard grads shifted 2:1 research → banking; now 1.5:1 banking → research
- **Manager proliferation:** More managers over last 30 years; *less* productivity and innovation (studies cited but not named in text)

---

### **Speenhamland Myth (Chapter 6)**

**Claim:** Opposition to basic income rests on a 200-year-old lie about an English welfare experiment.

**Evidence:**
- **Speenhamland (1795–1834):** Magistrates in southern England supplemented wages of poor to subsistence level (early basic income)
- **Royal Commission Report (1834):**
  - Claimed Speenhamland caused population explosion, wage collapse, moral decay  
  - Report was **fabricated:** conclusions written before data collected; only 10% of surveys returned; leading questions; interviewees were elites, not beneficiaries
- **Modern historians (1960s–70s re-analysis):**
  - Population growth was due to child labor demand (kids = income), not welfare
  - No poverty trap; wages didn't collapse because of Speenhamland
  - Agricultural productivity rose 33% (1790–1830)
  - Real problem: gold standard (advised by Ricardo) + threshing machines destroyed jobs
- **1834 replacement (workhouses):**
  - Heinous: families separated, forced labor, starvation  
  - Andover workhouse: inmates gnawed bones meant for fertilizer
- **Nixon (1969):**
  - Advisor Martin Anderson gave Nixon 6-page brief on Speenhamland myth  
  - Changed Nixon's rhetoric from "right to income" → "workfare" (despite not believing in work requirement himself)
  - Poisoned well for UBI for next 50 years

### **Randomistas & Development Aid (Chapter 9)**

**Claim:** Most development aid is based on ideology and guesswork; RCTs reveal what actually works.

**Evidence:**
- **Esther Duflo & MIT Poverty Action Lab (founded 2003):**
  - Conducted 500+ RCTs in 56 countries
- **Free textbooks (Kenya, Kremer 1998):**
  - First-ever RCT of development aid  
  - 25 schools got books, 25 didn't  
  - Result: **no improvement** in test scores (books were in English; kids couldn't read English well enough)
- **Mosquito nets (Kenya):**
  - Sachs: give them away free (in his "$10 Solution" paper)  
  - Easterly: sell them (free = dependency, local retailers destroyed)  
  - **RCT verdict:** Free distribution vastly increases uptake; 90% use nets correctly; people who got free nets are *more* (not less) likely to buy another net later
- **Deworming:** $10 treatment → +2.9 years schooling (best ROI found)
- **Microcredit (Hyderabad, India):** Duflo RCT showed **no impact** on poverty or health (despite hype)
- **Cash transfers:** Extensively studied worldwide; consistently outperform in-kind aid and training programs

**Implication:** Even well-meaning "experts" are often wrong; need rigorous testing; **but** migration would dwarf all aid gains.

## **FINAL CLAIM: Ideas Govern the World**

**Keynes (closing quote of book):**  
> "The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed, the world is ruled by little else."

**Hayek (paraphrase):**  
> Few believe ideas and beliefs are the main movers of history, but "the world is ruled by little else."

**Friedman's playbook:**
1. Develop alternative ideas when they're "politically impossible"
2. Keep them "lying around"
3. Wait for crisis
4. Implement rapidly

**Bregman's argument:**
- We *have* the crises (inequality, climate, meaninglessness, automation)
- We *lack* the fully developed alternatives
- The alternatives exist in prototype (Mincome, Kellogg's 6-hour day, open borders pre-WWI, Housing First, Cherokee casino, etc.)
- **We need to build the intellectual/political infrastructure NOW so the ideas are ready when the next breakdown comes**
